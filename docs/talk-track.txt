I. ARCHITECTURE — Let's talk about building blocks and architecture for transformer models over the next nine slides

The transformer block or transformer architecture is at the heart of modern foundation models. Everything we'll talk about pivots around a seminal paper called "Attention is all you need", written in 2017.

But, let's start with the basics. 

**Slide 2** — the **perceptron** and feed-forward neural networks--the FFNN. This is the basic building block for the AI models that we are all using today.

You may also hear these FFNN's called multi-layer perceptrons and they are directly connected to Frank Rosenblatt's work from the late 50's, which is itself inspired by bio/neuro work from the turn of the century  and research by McCullough and Pitts in the 1940's.

Neural networks are so named because they model human neurons (called nodes/activations in model-world) and the architecture of moderal AI systems connects many nodes into a network to process data.

The nodes are connected by weights and biases. I'll draw a quick diagram of that here.

**Slide 3** — 
For slide 3 and 4 I want to talk about a couple core primatives that go into building neural networks. 

So models don't read text directly. They work on **tokens**: numeric IDs for pieces of words (e.g. "Ingenious" → In, gen, ious). 

Tokenization is why models struggle with counting letters or spelling backwards — they're blind to individual characters.

**Slide 4** — those token IDs get turned into **embeddings**: vectors in a multi-dimensional **latent space** where similar meanings sit close together. 

Positional encodings add word order so "dog bites man" and "man bites dog" aren't the same.

Tokens and embeddings are why we can generalize across different media types (text, audio, visual) and languages.

Slides 5–6: 
Before 2017, if you wanted to process language, you used Recurrent Neural Networks (RNNs) or LSTMs. They were the first to add 'memory.' But they had a major hardware problem: they were processing data serially, one after another. 

To understand the 10th word, you had to process the first nine in order. This created a 'vanishing gradient'—by the time the model got to the end of a long sentence, it had effectively forgotten how it started.

You couldn't just throw more GPUs at the problem because the math was stuck in a line.

In 2014, we saw the 'Encoder-Decoder' breakthrough. This is what gave us Google Translate. It tried to compress an entire sentence into a single 'Thought Vector.' But as sentences got longer, that vector became a bottleneck—it’s hard to summarize War and Peace in one mathematical point. 

This is where 'Attention' was first born. It was a "crutch" for the RNNs and LSTMs of the time.

Then came 2017 and the 'Attention is All You Need' paper. The 'Eureka' moment was realizing we could delete the RNN entirely. By using Self-Attention, every token could look at every other token simultaneously. 

This unlocked massive parallelization. We could finally use thousands of GPUs to train on the entire internet at once.

The self-attention mechanism is a longer-talk on its own. But it is a way for all the vector to understand the relationships and importance of all the other vectors in the prompt. And because the Attention mechanism is a "Causal" model you also had this "snowball effect" where by the time you reach the final token of your prompt, that single vector is the 'smartest' one in the room. It is the only one that has 'seen' the entire prompt and is ready to predict what comes next.

II. INFERENCE — What Happens When You Hit Send
[Slides 10–13 — ~2 minutes]

You've typed a message. What actually happens?

**Slide 10** — the system assembles a **prompt stack**: (1) **system prompt** (hidden instructions), (2) **conversation history** (re-sent every turn — the model has no memory), (3) **your message**. Weights are frozen; only **activations** hold the current conversation and vanish when the session ends.

**Slide 11** — **Preparing the input.** The full prompt is assembled, tokenized (Slide 3), then embedded and given positional encodings (Slide 4). That sequence of vectors is what enters the Transformer. Every turn re-processes everything; cost and latency scale with context length.

**Slide 12** — the **forward pass** and **autoregressive loop**. The vectors flow up through every Transformer layer. The final token's vector is turned into scores (logits) over the vocabulary; a token is chosen (Slide 13); that token is appended and the whole process repeats. One token at a time — that's why you see streaming. In practice, **KV caching** avoids recomputing the full history for each new token.

**Slide 13** — the **selection dice roll**. Logits go through **softmax** to become probabilities. **Temperature** is the dial: low = pick the safest token; high = more variation and creativity. Many models use **weight tying** — the same embedding table used at input is used (transposed) at output. The model doesn't "know" the answer; it assigns probabilities. If the most plausible next token is wrong, it'll pick it anyway — **plausibility, not truth**. That's the core of why hallucinations happen.

We've seen how the machine runs. Next we'll see how these machines were built.

III. TRAINING
[Slides 14–16 — ~2.5 minutes]

So how do those billions of weights get tuned in the first place? That's what training is.

**Pre-training** (Slide 14) is where the model acquires what we might loosely call general intelligence. Initially, we set random weights and biases and feed data to a neural network and ask it to make a prediction. That's it. The model sees "The cat sat on the..." and has to predict "mat." When it's wrong, the error travels backward through the network — a process called **backpropagation** — and the weights are nudged, just slightly, in the right direction.

Now do that billions of times, across trillions of tokens of text from books, the internet, research papers, code — and something remarkable happens. In order to get good at next-token prediction, the model has to learn grammar, facts, logic, reasoning, even some understanding of cause and effect. It's not explicitly taught any of those things. They emerge as a consequence of doing one thing — prediction — at extraordinary scale.

This process runs for months on thousands of GPUs and costs millions to hundreds of millions of dollars. What you get at the end is called a **base model** — which is knowledgeable but not particularly useful. It completes sentences. It rambles. It doesn't know how to have a conversation.

Slide 15: Post-Training — From Knowledge to Helpful Presence
"If pre-training gives the model its 'raw intelligence,' post-training is where we give it a personality, a set of manners, and a moral compass. We start with a base model that is brilliant but unruly—it doesn't know how to follow instructions; it just knows how to finish sentences. To fix this, we use three layers of refinement.

First is Supervised Fine-Tuning (SFT). This is the 'Master and Apprentice' phase. Humans write out thousands of ideal responses to specific prompts. The model learns to mimic this professional, helpful tone. It’s effective, but it doesn't scale well—you can't write a perfect answer for every possible question in the world.

To solve that, we used to rely on RLHF (Reinforcement Learning from Human Feedback). In this setup, humans rank multiple model answers—A is better than B. We then had to train a separate 'Teacher Model' (a Reward Model) to learn those human preferences and 'grade' the student model. It worked, but it was notoriously unstable and expensive to maintain.

By 2026, the industry has largely moved to DPO (Direct Preference Optimization). This is the breakthrough that has made the current generation of models feel so much more 'intuitive.' We’ve effectively cut out the middleman. We no longer need that separate 'Teacher Model' to grade the student. Instead, DPO allows us to optimize the model’s weights directly using human preference data. It is simpler, more stable, and allows the model to align with our intent much more precisely.

But there is a trade-off to keep in mind: Alignment is a tax on creativity. Every guardrail we put in—every time we teach the model to say 'I cannot answer that' for safety—we are narrowing its 'latent space' slightly. We are trading unbounded, raw capability for a system that is safe, predictable, and ready for professional use."

Two practical implications here. First, the training data has a cutoff — the model doesn't know about things that happened after it was trained, unless you tell it or give it a tool to look things up. Second — and Slide 16 covers this directly — the model inherits whatever biases, gaps, and skewed representations exist in its training data. It's a mirror of the text it was trained on, not an arbiter of truth. That matters especially in high-stakes domains like hiring, legal analysis, or healthcare. No amount of post-training alignment fully eliminates those underlying biases — it reduces harmful outputs, but critical evaluation and human oversight remain essential.

IV. RAG — Teaching a Frozen Model New Tricks
[Slide 17 — ~1.5 minutes]

Here's the practical problem. The model is frozen. Its training data has a cutoff. It knows nothing about your firm's documents, your portfolio companies, or anything that happened last month.

**RAG** — Retrieval-Augmented Generation (Slide 17) — is the main solution. Before the model generates a response, you retrieve relevant chunks and inject them into the prompt. You're not retraining the model; you're giving it better context at inference time.

The retrieval step uses **semantic search**: the same embeddings and latent space we saw in Slide 4. You turn document chunks into vectors and store them in a **vector database**. When a question comes in, you embed it and find the chunks whose vectors are closest in meaning. Not keyword match — **semantic match**. "What did we say about our exit timeline?" can find the right paragraph even if those exact words don't appear. Infrastructure is typically an embedding model plus a vector store (e.g. Pinecone, Weaviate, Chroma).

Practical implications: RAG reduces hallucinations, enables citations, and keeps knowledge current without retraining. Your private data stays in your system; it's injected at inference time and never enters the model's training data or "global brain." That's the primary way to use proprietary data safely with AI.

V. MULTIMODAL, TOOL USE, REASONING & AGENTS
[Slides 18–21 — ~2.5 minutes]

**Slide 18 — Multimodal.** Models now handle images, audio, and video as well as text. The idea is a **unified embedding space**: images become patches (like tokens), audio becomes spectrograms, and everything is projected into the same latent space. The Transformer doesn't care what the vectors represent — it just processes them. So vision, speech, and text share the same "flavor profile" idea from Slide 4. Examples: GPT-4V, Claude, Gemini (vision + text); Whisper (audio); DALL-E, Midjourney (image generation).

**Slide 19 — Tool use.** LLMs are prediction engines with frozen weights — they can't run a calculator or hit an API. **Tool use** (function calling) lets the model output a structured request; your system runs the tool and feeds the result back into the conversation. That extends the model beyond text: web search, code execution, database queries, booking APIs. Models can chain multiple tools in sequence — which is what makes agents possible.

---

VI. REASONING & AGENTS — From Chat to Action
[Slides 20–21 — ~2 minutes]

**Slide 20 — Reasoning: two paradigms.** **Chain-of-Thought (CoT)** — a prompting technique from 2022: ask the model to "think step by step." Visible reasoning, more output tokens, big gains on math and logic. **Inference-time compute** (2024–2025): reasoning built into the model. Models like o1 and DeepSeek-R1 generate hidden reasoning tokens — they "think" for seconds or minutes before answering. That's AI's version of System 2: slow, deliberate, effortful. CoT proved models can reason if given space; inference-time compute teaches them when and how to allocate that thinking automatically.

**Slide 21 — Agentic workflows.** An **agent** isn't a one-shot chatbot. It runs a **perception–reasoning–action loop**: observe, reason, act (via tools), observe the result, repeat until the goal is met. That pattern is referred to as System 2 thinking.

What are these tools? Currently, the tools are things like web search, code execution, databases, APIs. Frameworks like LangGraph, AutoGPT, CrewAI power coding assistants (Cursor, Devin), research tools, support bots. Agents need clear success criteria, error handling, and often human-in-the-loop for high-stakes tasks. They're costlier (many LLM calls per task) but can accomplish multi-step work autonomously.

VII. WHICH MODEL FOR WHAT — A Practical Selection Guide
[~1.5 minutes — Slide 22 Recap summarizes "when to use what"]

One of the most common practical questions I get is: which model should I be using? The answer has changed dramatically in the last twelve months and will keep changing — but here's a framework that holds up.

Think in three tiers based on what you're trading off: speed and cost against capability and reasoning depth.

**Tier 1 — The "Instant" Models (Reflex Thinking)**

*The Lineup:* GPT-5 mini, Gemini 3 Flash, Claude 4.5 Haiku.

*The Pitch:* These are the "sub-second" models. In 2026, we don't just use these for email categorization anymore; we use them as the "eyes and ears" of agentic loops. They are relatively cheap — fractions of a cent for thousands of tokens.

*The Warning:* They are still System 1 thinkers. They will "reflexively" answer even if they don't know the fact, leading to confident hallucinations in specialized domains.

**Tier 2 — The Workhorses (Professional Reasoning)**

*The Lineup:* GPT-5.2 Instant, Claude 4.5 Sonnet, Gemini 3.1 Pro.

*The Pitch:* This is your daily driver. These models have "native" reasoning that far surpasses the top-tier models of two years ago. Most professional coding (or "vibe coding") and complex document synthesis happen here. They balance speed with enough depth to follow complex, multi-page instructions without losing the thread.

*The Vibe:* If you aren't sure which to use, use these.

**Tier 3 — The "Deep Thinkers" (Inference-Time Compute)**

*The Lineup:* OpenAI o3-pro, Claude 4.6 (with Max Effort / Extended Thinking), Gemini 3.1 Pro (Reasoning Mode), DeepSeek-R1 (or R2).

*The Pitch:* These are the models that "stare at the wall" before answering. You use these when the cost of being wrong is higher than the cost of the compute. We're talking about Ph.D.-level science, hardened code architecture, or legal and financial strategy where the model needs to explore thousands of hidden paths to find the one that works.

*The Meta-Point:* You are no longer paying for the answer; you are paying for the silence while it thinks.

Slide 22 (**Recap**) sums it up: **when to use what** — prompt engineering (cheap, start here) → RAG (factual accuracy, private data, citations) → fine-tuning (specialized domains) → reasoning models (complex logic/math). Model size: smaller models are faster and cheaper but less capable; choose the smallest that meets your accuracy needs.

VIII. FRONTIER — What's Coming Next
[Slide 23 — Looking Ahead — ~1 minute]

Slide 23 points at what's already in production or in heavy research.

**JEPA** (Joint Embedding Predictive Architecture) — Yann LeCun's direction: predict in representation space, not tokens or pixels. Goal: world models, planning, sample-efficient learning. V-JEPA and I-JEPA are early implementations.

**Mamba & State Space Models (SSMs)** — linear or near-linear sequence complexity instead of quadratic attention. Long context with lower memory cost; used in some long-context and efficient LLMs.

**Mixture of Experts (MoE)** — route each token to a subset of "expert" sub-networks instead of one dense stack. Scale total parameters (e.g. 400B+) without scaling compute per token. DeepSeek-V3, Llama-4, Gemini-2.5, Mixtral use it.

**RWKV & RetNet** — RNN-like inference (O(1) memory) with parallelizable training. "Successor to Transformer" narrative; constant-memory decoding and long context. RWKV-7 and RetNet are in active use.

**Hybrids** — models that combine attention with SSMs and/or MoE (e.g. Jamba: attention + Mamba + MoE). "Attention was never enough"; combining mechanisms is a major trend.

We're not there yet. But these are the threads worth pulling.

I still believe we have decade's worth of activity just to incorporate everything that's been created as of last year but research continues at a break-neck speed.
